# Articulation Matrix: The 8-Week Camp and the NextGen FinTech Scholars Program

## How the two fit together

This intensive eight-week camp is the methodological and operational spine of the **NextGen FinTech Scholars / Data Science Young Scholars Research Program**, a summer research program at George Mason University's Schar School of Policy and Government that runs from **June 26 through September 11, 2026** and is directed by Dr. Lei Gao. NextGen is organized in three phases. **Phase 1** is seven weekly Friday meetings, 12:00–2:00 p.m. EST, from June 26 through August 7, that introduce FinTech as the meeting point of artificial intelligence, machine learning, and data analytics and that walk scholars through the professional data stack — CRSP, Compustat, SEC EDGAR, Bloomberg — alongside AI working partners (Claude, ChatGPT). The program then carries scholars through a conference presentation in mid-August and a paper-refinement window that ends with a final paper submitted to the Schar School *Young Scholars Journal* and deposited in GMU's MARS research repository. Scholarship awards up to $2,000 are tied to the strongest finished work. The camp is the intensive edition of that arc: it compresses the whole journey — methods, project build, presentation, and submission — into eight weeks, folding the conference talk, the poster, the defense, and the submission packet into its final two weeks rather than spreading them across a longer refinement tail.

The camp and NextGen are deliberately complementary rather than redundant. NextGen leads on cohort, context, faculty access, and the published-paper venue. The camp leads on what a serious empirical paper actually needs underneath: a defensible research design, an honest standard-error story, a replication package that runs end-to-end, and the reading habits that let a student tell a finding from a coincidence. A student who completes the camp arrives in NextGen already able to do the hard part well. A student in both gets the rare combination of a real research question, the methods to answer it honestly, and a venue that publishes the answer.

## What NextGen brings that the camp does not

The camp can take a student a long way alone, but five things only NextGen provides, and all five are load-bearing. The **cohort** matters most: NextGen places a scholar in a team of similarly motivated high-school students who must argue about a research question, divide labor, and defend the result together — an experience no textbook delivers. The **live faculty mentorship** is second: seven Friday afternoons of guided discussion with Dr. Gao and invited speakers, plus the question-and-answer around the August 14 presentation, give feedback on a student's own work that no static curriculum can substitute for. The **$2,000 scholarship awards**, tied to the strongest finished work, create real stakes that turn "summer project" into "submitted paper." The **publication and archival venue** is fourth: a finished NextGen paper appears in the Schar School *Young Scholars Journal* and is deposited in GMU's MARS research repository, giving the work a permanent, citable home that follows the student into college applications and beyond. **Conference networking** — with faculty, industry guests, and the cohort itself — is the fifth.

## What the camp brings that NextGen by itself does not

NextGen's seven Friday meetings, conference, and refinement window are an excellent scaffold, but they assume a methodological maturity that high-school students rarely arrive with. The camp builds that maturity, in full. It provides a **structured, intensive eight-week curriculum** mapped onto the NextGen calendar, with chapters and reading guides that take a student from probability and the logic of inference through OLS in matrix form, causal inference, panel-data and natural-experiment designs, the robustness frontier, text-as-data, replication, the manuscript build, presentation, and submission. Each chapter ships a **runnable notebook** — **forty in total, across Weeks 1–8** — that enforces the camp's reproducibility discipline (`python=3.11`, pinned `pandas>=2.2`, `numpy`, `scipy`, `statsmodels`, `linearmodels`, `pyfixest`, `matplotlib`, `sklearn`; seeded RNG; `matplotlib.use("Agg")`; no chained indexing without `.copy()`). It provides **full solution manuals** for every problem set in Appendix E, so a student can actually practice the moves rather than admire them, drawing on **34 data cards** that document each public source end-to-end. It includes **five capstone exemplars** in the capstone gallery — full, faculty-grade research write-ups in the camp's house style, one for each member of the recurring student cast — that show what a finished NextGen paper should look like. It teaches the **AI co-pilot module**, an explicit, citation-disciplined protocol for using Claude and ChatGPT in empirical research without surrendering authorship or rigor. And it embeds **bias-and-robustness drills** — placebo tests, leave-one-out re-estimation, alternative clustering, alternative samples, multiple-testing corrections, and the threats-to-validity audit — into every empirical chapter, so robustness is a habit rather than a one-time appendix exercise.

## The handshake

A student moves through both programs the way a serious researcher moves through a real project. The first seven camp weeks march in lockstep with the seven Phase 1 Fridays: each Friday at NextGen is the live application of what the camp covered Monday through Thursday, anchored on a published Gao paper that models the move. By Week 7 the student already has a written specification, a populated GitHub repository, a first-draft results table, and a manuscript draft with publication-grade tables and figures; the NextGen team adopts that backbone and extends it on professional data. Week 8 is the public test and the finish: the analysis is run and stress-tested, then presented — the talk, the poster, and the Q&A defense — and submitted to the *Young Scholars Journal* with a MARS deposit, a public replication package, and a signed reproducibility statement, with the final submission landing by **Friday, September 11, 2026**. The longer, slower version of that final stretch — a dedicated symposium week and a four-week revise-and-resubmit cycle — is available on demand in office hours for students who want to keep refining after the camp's eight weeks close. The camp gets the science right; NextGen gets the science published; together they produce a high-school researcher whose first paper is genuinely defensible and genuinely citable, in a venue that will still be there in ten years.
