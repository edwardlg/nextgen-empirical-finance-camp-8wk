# Ch 8.7 — Submission & the Long Arc

The previous chapter put you on a stage and beside a poster: eight minutes, a hostile question, a folder a stranger can rebuild your numbers from. That was the last act of the camp's private life — the room is people you know, the clock is short, and when it stops, the paper is still yours alone. This chapter is about what happens when you hand the paper to the world: you submit it into the public record, post it where strangers can find it, and start the long, often silent process by which a result becomes a published paper. Maya, Devon, Priya, Sam, and Leah each walk out of the defense with a manuscript they can finally read end-to-end without flinching. The work, in one sense, is done. In another, it is just beginning, because a paper nobody has submitted or posted is a private object — and the point of research is to put a claim where others can check it, cite it, and build on it.

Here is the move the whole chapter turns on. **Submission is not attaching a PDF to a form; it is the first move in a multi-year arc — the time-stamp, the credential, the entry into a conversation that outlasts the camp — and the discipline that makes you a researcher is doing that first move cleanly and then surviving everything after it.** The last chapter was about being *believed* in a room; this one is about being *findable, citable, and durable* outside one, and about the posture toward silence and rejection that decides whether these eight weeks stay alive or fade into a single-summer memory. We cover, in order: the submission packet for the Schar Young Scholars Journal and MARS, the SSRN preprint, the decade-long arc from poster to top journal, the conference circuit, and what comes after submission — the reviewer wait, the desk-reject, the revise-and-resubmit.

## 1. The submission packet: Schar Young Scholars Journal and MARS

A submission is not one file; it is a **packet** of five components, each for a different reader. Most first-time submitters write the manuscript carefully and treat the other four as boilerplate — the asymmetric mistake, because the modal reason a submission gets bounced is procedural ("incomplete packet"), not substantive: roughly one in seven first-time submissions is returned because the paperwork, not the science, is bad. The packet satisfies three readers at once — the **editor** doing a three-minute intake check, the **archivist** ingesting metadata, and the **future-you of 2030** who must defend authorship, funding, and contributions in a tenure file or grad-school application.

The five components, in the order the editor reads them:

1. **The manuscript** — one PDF typeset to publication standard: double-spaced, page numbers, line numbers (referees cite them), tables and figures near first reference, references in house style (the Schar Young Scholars Journal uses Chicago author-date).
2. **The cover letter** — under 400 words, four paragraphs: *identification* (paper, category, corresponding author), *contribution* (one or two sentences — the compressed introduction claim, not a re-run of the abstract), *fit* (why this venue, in the journal's own scope language), *attestations* (originality and exclusive-submission, plus three suggested reviewers, none you have a conflict with).
3. **The author statement** — the CRediT taxonomy (the [Contributor Roles Taxonomy](https://credit.niso.org/) maintained by NISO): fourteen standardized roles (*Conceptualization, Data curation, Formal analysis, Funding acquisition, Investigation, Methodology, Project administration, Resources, Software, Supervision, Validation, Visualization, Writing — original draft, Writing — review and editing*) with intensity descriptors (lead / equal / supporting). Every role gets at least one author; none is unattributed; the corresponding author is named.
4. **The data-and-code availability statement** — every source by proper noun, its license (public / restricted / proprietary), where the cleaned dataset and code live, and the repository handle. The Schar journal follows [DA-RT](http://www.dartstatement.org/); the phrase *"available from the author on request"* is now read as *"not available"* (authors fulfill such requests less than 20% of the time — King 1995; Vlaeminck 2013; Christensen and Miguel 2018). Write the deposit instead.
5. **The conflicts-of-interest declaration** — usually one sentence for a high-school author. For Prof. Gao as mentor co-author: the structural conflict (the mentor relationship), the compensation arrangement, the absence of substantive conflicts — disclosed honestly and short.

The CRediT statement is the one students write hastily and should not. Its real function is conflict-prevention: it forces the *who did what* conversation while memories are fresh, rather than years later when "what was your specific contribution?" becomes an argument. The other camp students are *acknowledged but not co-authored* — lab-session feedback is helpful but does not meet the [ICMJE](http://www.icmje.org/recommendations/) criteria. The temptation to over-assign credit to the student, out of generosity, is real; the discipline is honesty — if the mentor ran the regressions, the student is not *lead* on Formal analysis.

**Two channels, not one.** The **Schar Young Scholars Journal** is the in-house peer-reviewed undergraduate-and-precollege venue at the GMU Schar School and affiliated Costello College of Business; if accepted, the paper appears in a numbered issue with a citable DOI (the `https://doi.org/...` string registered with Crossref that makes a paper formally citable). The **MARS repository** (Mason Archival Repository System, hosted by GMU Libraries) is an *archive*, not a journal: deposit, librarians ingest, and within 5–10 business days the paper gets a permanent *handle* (`https://hdl.handle.net/1920/xxxxx`) and becomes discoverable through Google Scholar and the OAI-PMH harvest network — a stable identifier, though not a DOI. Most camp papers go to **both** (journal for the credential and DOI, MARS for permanence), and a student even *possibly* continuing should add SSRN. Submission runs through ScholarOne and DSpace (Dublin Core metadata); both go faster with all five components finalized first. Submit Friday afternoon, not Sunday night, so intake lands Monday fresh; verify (PDF opens cleanly, counts match, references resolve, the cover letter names the right journal); then close the laptop. There is no news for at least a week.

## 2. The SSRN preprint: posting, versioning, the DOI question

The week you submit, do one more thing: **post the paper to SSRN as a working paper.** A preprint is not a duplicate publication; it is a *time-stamped public claim to a result*, and for a precollege author whose career has not started, that may be the more important credential. The journal article is the *citation*; the preprint is the *resume entry* — and it arrives earlier and lasts longer. When Maya applies to college or a PhD program in 2027, the committee can verify an October-2026 SSRN posting in ninety seconds; a "forthcoming in the Schar Young Scholars Journal" credential is unverifiable until the article appears, possibly after the deadline.

A preprint serves three functions. **Priority** — SSRN time-stamps to the minute, so a faculty member who runs the same regression and posts a month later has to cite you. **Dissemination** — posting emails the abstract to the networks you choose (the Financial Economics Network, FEN, ~35,000 subscribers; the Banking Network, BankN, ~8,000), entering the conversation in days rather than the eighteen months a journal takes. **The resume credential** — a durable, verifiable record that you finished a project others download.

SSRN, the Social Science Research Network (Elsevier-owned since 2016), is the dominant working-paper venue in empirical finance. It is *not* a journal (no peer review; intake rejects ~5% for scope) and *not* an archive (papers are mutable — the source of its one pathology). The workflow is short: register with an ORCID (the identifier that unifies you across SSRN, MARS, ORCID, and Google Scholar), enter the abstract and 5–10 keywords, add JEL codes (Maya: G21, G28, J15, R51), choose three-to-five networks where readers would actually care, and upload. Cross-post to FEN plus two-to-four bespoke secondaries (Maya: FEN, BankN, the Discrimination Law eJournal); ten reads as desperate and dilutes attention.

**The DOI question** is the one students ask most. Does SSRN register a DOI? *Sometimes, conditionally.* For most independent working papers — Maya's included — **no**: the paper gets a persistent URL (`https://ssrn.com/abstract=NNNN`) but not a Crossref-registered DOI. Papers posted through a registered series (Harvard Business School, NBER-mirrored) do. This is fine — Maya's paper carries three persistent identifiers: the SSRN URL (the preprint's citation), the MARS handle (the data-and-code citation), and eventually the journal DOI. Name the MARS handle in the SSRN "Note" field so anyone landing there finds the data and code.

**Versioning discipline** prevents the worst preprint failure — the "which version did you read?" problem. Preprints are mutable and the abstract page shows the latest version. Four rules: (1) every version gets a dated number on the title page (*"Version 1.0, October 4, 2026"*); (2) significant revisions — a changed coefficient, sample, or conclusion — get a *new* cross-linked posting, not a silent file swap; (3) **never silently change a coefficient** — log it, because a referee who saw the old numbers trusts you *more* when the change is disclosed; (4) keep old versions downloadable. A footnote-plus-changelog makes a mutable preprint as trustworthy as an immutable deposit. The only exceptions to *post immediately*: a confidentiality constraint in a data license, a co-author who has not consented in writing, a target journal that prohibits preprints (rare — JF, JFE, RFS, AER, QJE all permit; check [SHERPA RoMEO](https://v2.sherpa.ac.uk/romeo/)), or a paper that is not finished. For Maya, all clear. Post.

## 3. The decade-long arc: from poster to *Journal of Finance*

Some camp papers stop at the Schar submission — the result was solid, the credential earned, the student moves on. The unusual ones keep going, and it helps to see how far "keep going" runs. **The journey from a working-paper draft to a top-journal publication is not a straight line; it is a seven-to-twelve-year sequence of revisions, conferences, rejections, and reworkings that bears almost no resemblance to the journey from a course paper to a course grade.** Papers that publish at top venues typically absorb two or three desk-rejects, three to five referee rounds across multiple journals, and dozens of conference presentations, emerging *recognizably the same paper* but in important respects a different one.

The honest case study is Prof. Gao's own in-progress paper — *Rainbow of Credits: Municipal Borrowing, Color Segments, and the Hidden Politics of Local Finance*, by Lei Gao, Sicong Liu, and Yu Wang, on whether U.S. counties' political-ideological composition affects municipal borrowing after controlling for fiscal fundamentals. As of 2026 it has been presented at AEA 2025 and is being prepared for submission to the *Journal of Finance*. It is **not yet published** and the arc is **not yet complete** — which is the point. Most methods-book case studies are retrospective and clean; real research is messy in the middle, and the middle is where the lessons are.

The phases run roughly:

- **Phase 1 — Idea (months 0–6).** The first version of an idea is usually three ideas. The Rainbow draft conflated borrowing levels, costs, and composition; the breakthrough was a colleague's "There are three papers here. Pick one." They picked levels. *Narrowing* is the discipline.
- **Phase 2 — First circulation (months 6–18).** First SSRN posting, first brown-bag, first advisor feedback. The paper is named, dated, claimed for priority; major rewrites begin.
- **Phase 3 — Conference presentations (months 12–36).** Five to fifteen presentations over two years, each reshaping the next revision. Rainbow's discussants demanded a multi-dimensional political-composition measure, an institutional-feature classification, a Canadian-provincial external-validity check, a mechanism decomposition, and a title defense ("too cute — you'll lose the editor"). The paper grew from 38 to 62 pages.
- **Phase 4 — First journal submission (months 24–36).** Choose the **stretch journal** — one notch above where you think it "should" land — and aim high. Rainbow targets JF; the ladder beneath is JFE, then RFS, then the next tier (*Review of Finance, Journal of Financial and Quantitative Analysis, Journal of Banking and Finance*).
- **Phase 5 — The R&R cycle (months 36–84).** One to three revise-and-resubmit rounds, each 12–18 months, each demanding a 30–50-page response letter. The slowest, most invisible, most uncertain phase.
- **Phase 6 — Acceptance and proof (months 72–96).** Copy-editing, proofs, the DOI registered when the article goes online.
- **Phase 7 — Citation and the long tail.** Citations peak in the first 24 months, then settle. (Gao's earlier intraday-momentum paper passed 800 citations by 2023 because it touched an active subliterature.)

The arc is non-linear: conferences run *during* R&R, journals run serially (each rejection adds 8–12 months), papers split and merge and get renamed, and methodological shifts force rework — Rainbow's strategy was partly redone when the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) staggered-DiD literature reset the standards, costing nine months. Some papers run a **shorter arc** (three to five years) when identification is unusually clean, the topic hot, or the author established. Maya's fair-lending paper has clean identification (staggered DiD on a regulatory rollout) and a moderately hot topic, so a *Journal of Financial Economics* or *Journal of Banking and Finance* placement in her college years is feasible. The honest odds for any single ambitious paper: perhaps 50% at the top target within seven years, 35% lower, 15% never. Hence the load-bearing lesson — **portfolios beat individual papers.** No single paper carries a career; keep several in flight at different phases so one paper's slow stretch is offset by another's progress.

## 4. The conference circuit: FMA, AEA, SFS Cavalcade

Phase 3 runs through conferences, and the circuit is not one thing — it is a **stratified ecosystem** of venues with different audiences, selection standards, and rewards. The discipline is to enter at the level you are ready for and climb deliberately. A presentation serves four functions: **feedback** (dominant for an early-stage paper), **credentialing** (a resume line whose value comes from the selection rate), **networking** (invisible short-term, dominant long-term), and **visibility** (feeding the citation long tail).

The hierarchy runs from most-selective to most-accessible. The **majors' main programs** (AEA, AFA, WFA, EFA; 12–30% acceptance) are not yet open to precollege presenters, but their **undergraduate/pipeline tracks** are — chiefly the **AEA Summer Pipeline Program** (a competitive 8-week residency for rising college juniors/seniors, *the* canonical credentialing program for underrepresented students entering economics PhDs; the affiliated AEA Mentoring Program through CSMGEP reaches high-schoolers). The **second-tier nationals** include the **SFS Cavalcade** (the Society for Financial Studies conference; the SFS publishes the *Review of Financial Studies*) — ~12% main program, with an Undergraduate Research Symposium (~35%) beneath it whose discussant feedback is among the best anywhere. The **regionals** (Eastern, Midwest, Southwestern, Western FMAs; the Eastern, Southern, Western economics associations; 40–70%) are *the workhorse venues*. The **undergraduate-specific** entry tier is led by the **FMA Undergraduate Poster Session** (the canonical first venue) and the Carroll Round Conference at Georgetown (~12%, explicitly considers high-school authors). The **high-school-specific** venues (Regeneron Science Talent Search, the Junior Science and Humanities Symposium, the Schar Young Scholars Symposium) are supplementary to the research-career path, not substitutive.

The plan for a camp graduate is to climb deliberately: the FMA Undergraduate Poster the fall after camp, a regional by spring of freshman year, the SFS Cavalcade undergraduate symposium by junior year with the second or third paper, the AEA Pipeline by senior year, the majors' main programs in graduate school. The **FMA Undergraduate Poster Session** is the must-do credential: submit an extended abstract in mid-June, present a 36-by-48-inch poster (six panels — title, abstract, question, data and design, results, implications) in October, and give the same 90-second pitch fifteen-to-twenty times while judges score a rubric — the credential plus 90 minutes of dense feedback. Maya's eighteen-month plan: FMA poster (October 2026), attend the AEA as a registered student in January 2027 (the highest-value five-day window even without presenting), a regional in spring, the Carroll Round application in February, the SFS Cavalcade undergraduate symposium by 2028. The four workhorses are the FMA poster, the Carroll Round, the AEA-to-attend, and one regional; the camp's alumni-support fund underwrites two-to-three.

The structural feature that distinguishes academic conferences is the **discussant** — a faculty member assigned to engage your paper formally before the audience. When the discussant speaks, your job is to **listen** — not defend, not interrupt, record every point in your notebook (not on your face) — then take your 1–2 minutes to acknowledge what is right, push back gently on what is mistaken, and commit to following up. The audience judges your judgment, not your debating skills. The discussant's main critique often becomes a referee's main critique six months later, so addressing it now is months of work saved. Save the written comments, treat them as a referee report, and send a short, specific thank-you within 48 hours — the person who discussed your paper in 2027 may referee it, or recommend you, in 2031.

## 5. After submission: the reviewer wait, the desk-reject, the R&R

The day you submit, the next thing that happens is **silence**. The manuscript-number email confirms receipt within an hour; then nothing, for weeks. The silence is structural, not personal: the editor reads within five business days and either desk-rejects or assigns referees (~three weeks); referees take 8–12 weeks; synthesis another 2–4. Total to first decision: 14–22 weeks, ~16 for the Schar journal (faster than most adult journals' 24). You have approximately zero leverage — you cannot speed the editor, prompt the referees, or improve the locked version. The emotional rhythm is predictable (relief, curiosity, anxiety, the tenth-week temptation to email "to check on status," which editors mildly resent), and the one practice that helps is **do other work**: prepare the FMA poster, attend the AEA, draft the Carroll Round application, start the next project. The wait is time to invest in the rest of the portfolio.

The first decision is one of four outcomes. **Acceptance** (full or conditional) is rare on first submission (~5%, essentially nonexistent at top journals). **Revise-and-resubmit** is the most desirable common outcome and the publication mechanism of empirical finance — interested editor, substantial revision wanted, referee reports attached. **Reject after review** is a "soft" no, but the reports are valuable; absorb them and submit elsewhere. **Desk-reject** is the "hard" no — returned without referees, the most common outcome at top journals (~40% at JF). For Maya's Schar submission the modal outcome is R&R (the journal's developmental mission favors it); for an adult-journal target like Rainbow, the modal first outcome is a desk-reject — *not a failure of the paper, but the modal output of the system.*

**Absorbing a desk-reject** is hard because the feedback is minimal — often three sentences, "not a good fit at this time." The moves: do *not* respond emotionally; parse what little there is against the journal's recent issues; **send the paper to the next venue without delay** (have a stretch → next-tier → safety ladder set *before* the first decision, so the reject triggers no paralysis); learn *in aggregate* (one is noise, three in a row is signal — ask two trusted readers "what are editors seeing that I'm not?"); and do not let any single reject change your view of the paper's value (many top-journal papers had several — Krueger and Mas (2004) on tire recalls before QJE; the Bhattacharya, Galpin, and Haslag (2020) high-frequency-trading paper desk-rejected three times before RFS). **The R&R** is the most consequential outcome short of acceptance — an *option to continue*, judged at a stricter standard the second round because the referees have now invested. The central artifact is the **response letter**, which for a top-finance R&R runs 30–80 pages, sometimes longer than the manuscript: a cover summary, then a point-by-point response to the editor and to each referee, quoting every comment verbatim and naming the section, page, and table where the change appears. The principles: quote exactly; answer with a *specific* change ("re-estimated with state-level clustering; the point estimate is unchanged, the t-statistic falls from 4.7 to 3.3 but stays significant at 1%"), never a vague "we have addressed this"; **disagree when you should, but carefully** — agreeing with everything is weakness, not strength; be honest about the infeasible and offer the feasible substitute; never surface antagonism, because the editor reads the tone. Budget a 6-month R&R as: weeks 1–2 read the reports twice and find each referee's *main* critique; weeks 3–4 triage (the Ch 8.4 feedback-ledger discipline at full scale); weeks 5–12 do the empirical work; weeks 13–16 rewrite; weeks 17–22 write the response letter; weeks 23–24 verify and resubmit.

## 6. The five-year research identity

Step back from the single paper. **The reviewer wait, the desk-reject, the R&R, and the eventual acceptance are not events that happen to your paper; they happen to *you*, and what makes you a researcher five years from now is how you respond to them.** A paper is a single artifact; a research identity is a multi-year posture toward the silence you do not yet know how to wait through and the rejection you do not yet know how to absorb. The camp has been training that posture all summer; here it assembles into a sustained practice.

Six practices sustain the long work: **read three papers a week** (depth, not volume; ~750 over five years, the substrate of a career); **write 30 minutes a day** (routine, not inspiration, before email); **present at one conference a year, minimum** (the feedback engine); **maintain the portfolio** (one paper in late revision, one in early circulation, one as an idea, so no single project carries the load); **build relationships** (academic finance is small — the discussant of 2027 is the referee of 2031); and **keep a research journal** so you still remember why you cared. And cultivate the mentor past Week 8: a light-touch email twice a year (October and April) keeps the thread that recommendation letters and your first job will hang on.

Ask the five-year question Prof. Gao asks in the final lab: *what do you want to be working on in five years — a substantive area, a methodological identity, a venue identity?* The answer can change every six months, but have one today. Maya's in June 2026: consumer-credit access, applied-microeconometric methods, finance and economics journals. Five years on she may want insurance markets, or crypto, or something unnamed — and that is fine. Articulating the plan is what *creates* the identity; the specifics evolve.

Look at where Week 1 started — a series of returns and a mean of about eight percent — and trace what the eight weeks walked: a number became a number-with-uncertainty; "we control for X" became a *claim*, not a spell; claims became *designs* you can defend sentence by sentence; you made an idea *yours*, pre-registered it, built it without look-ahead, named every threat; and in Week 8 you made it robust, peer-reviewed, defended in eight minutes, packaged so a stranger can rebuild it with one command — and now submitted, posted, and pointed down the long arc. What you have is not "the average return was 8%." It is a question nobody had cleanly answered, attacked with a defensible design, estimated with honest uncertainty, reproducible by one command, and now *out in the world* — an original empirical paper, the first move in a portfolio that, sustained, becomes a body of work. The camp does not require that you become an academic researcher; most of you will go on to other fields, and the experience of having *done the work*, question to submission, is yours regardless. For those who continue: the work compounds. This paper is the first; the conference circuit is the next move; the R&R cycle the third; the published paper the fourth.

The book ends here. The research career begins. Both are good things. Go submit.

## Your Turn

Two extensions close the camp — the same arc seen from its two ends, the first move and the long horizon.

- **The submission packet, assembled and verified.** Build all five components to publication standard: the typeset manuscript, the under-400-word four-paragraph cover letter (named to the *right* journal), the complete CRediT statement (every role assigned, honest intensity descriptors, corresponding author named), the data-and-code availability statement (every source by proper noun, the repository handle, and *not one instance* of "available from the author on request" — grep for it), and the conflicts declaration. Then plan the two-channel submission (Schar Young Scholars Journal + MARS) and the SSRN posting (ORCID linked, JEL codes set, three-to-five networks each with a reason, the MARS handle in the Note field, a dated Version 1.0 footnote), and run the Friday-afternoon verification before you click.
- **The five-year plan, written down.** Write your answer to Prof. Gao's final-lab question — a substantive area, a methodological identity, a venue identity — and beside it the six sustaining practices (three papers a week, 30 minutes a day, one conference a year, two projects in the portfolio, two mentor emails a year, a research journal opened this week) as a checklist to re-run every semester for five years. The plan can change every six months; the point is to have one today.

Then turn to the **capstone gallery** in `book/capstones/` and the **front matter** that opened this book: five finished papers, each the full-size version of the object you just learned to submit. Read one not for its result but for its *arc* — the question it claims, the identification it defends, the honest limit it admits — and ask the question this whole camp was pointed at: *if a stranger clones the repo and types one command, do they get the number, and if they search SSRN in three years, do they find the author?* When both answers are yes, you are not finished — you are launched.
