**Reading a Patent's Worth Off the Stock Price: A KPSS-Style Innovation Measure and a Grant-Date Event Study**

*Leah R., NextGen FinTech Scholars / Data Science Young Scholars Research Program*

## Abstract

How much is a single invention worth? Patent counts treat a trivial design tweak and the transistor as one unit each; forward citations are slower and arrive a decade too late to value a recent patent. Following Kogan, Papanikolaou, Seru & Stoffman (2017), I build a market-based, dollar-denominated innovation measure: the abnormal stock return in a three-day window around a patent's grant date, scaled by market capitalization, read as the market's real-time estimate of the patent's private value. Working in a seeded **synthetic** panel of 60 firms and 633 patents over 2018–2021 — where the true patent value is known by construction — I reconstruct the measure with a Week-4 market-model event study and then run two tests. First, a *recovery* check: the reconstructed value correlates with the planted true value at 0.57 in levels and 0.38 in logs at the patent level, rising to 0.72 once aggregated to the firm-year, because averaging cancels the idiosyncratic window noise. Second, a *predictive* check: regressing next-year firm profitability on this year's log innovation value yields a positive, significant coefficient ($\hat\beta = 0.0054$, $t = 3.6$), the same validation pattern KPSS report on real data. A contamination experiment — injecting simulated earnings surprises into a rising share of grant windows — degrades both the recovery correlation (0.38 to 0.25) and the predictive $t$-statistic (3.6 to 2.5), making visible the confounding-news threat that the short window and firm-year aggregation are designed to blunt. The exercise demonstrates how to turn an unobservable (the economic value of an idea) into an observable, and exactly where the construction can break.

## 1. Introduction

The single most cited patent of the 2010s and a patent that protects a slightly rounder bottle cap both count as "one patent." That is the problem that has dogged the economics of innovation since it began: the obvious way to measure how much a firm invents — count its patents — weights a breakthrough and a triviality equally, and the standard fix — count the forward citations a patent later attracts — is both slow, since citations accrue over ten or more years, and truncated, since a patent granted last year looks uncited only because the future has not happened yet. Neither proxy is denominated in the unit anyone making a capital-allocation decision actually cares about, which is dollars. An R&D manager, an investor, or a policymaker wants to know what an invention is *worth*, not how many of them there are.

This paper builds and stress-tests a dollar-denominated innovation measure, in the spirit of Kogan, Papanikolaou, Seru & Stoffman (2017, hereafter KPSS), on a synthetic dataset where I know the right answer. **The contribution is pedagogical and methodological: I demonstrate, against a known ground truth, that a patent's private economic value can be read off the stock-market reaction to its grant, that the resulting firm-year measure predicts future profitability, and that confounding news in the grant window degrades the measure in a direction and magnitude I can quantify.** Because the data are simulated with a planted true value, I can do what no empirical paper can — compare the constructed measure to the truth it is trying to capture — which is the whole point of building it in a sandbox before trusting it in the wild.

I find three things. First, the event-study reconstruction *recovers* the planted patent value: the correlation between estimated and true value is 0.57 in levels and 0.38 in logs across patents, and climbs to 0.72 when patents are aggregated to a firm-year panel, because firm-year sums average out the day-to-day idiosyncratic noise that contaminates any single three-day window. Second, the measure *validates* the way KPSS's does: regressing next-year firm profitability on this year's log innovation value gives a positive and statistically significant coefficient ($t = 3.6$), tracking the same predictive relationship the published measure exhibits on real firms. Third, the measure *breaks* in a legible way: when I contaminate a growing fraction of grant windows with simulated earnings-announcement returns, both the recovery correlation and the predictive $t$-statistic fall monotonically, exposing the confounding-window threat that is the central vulnerability of the design.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 positions the measure against the patent-counting and citation literature and states what KPSS added. Section 3 describes the synthetic data and the real-data pull it stands in for. Section 4 lays out the event-study design, states the identifying assumption in one sentence, and tabulates the threats to it. Section 5 reports the headline recovery and validation results. Section 6 runs the robustness checks — window length, a placebo window, and the contamination sweep. Section 7 concludes with the limits I cannot design away.

## 2. Positioning

The literature on measuring innovation is a history of progressively better proxies, each fixing a defect in the last. The first generation counts **patents**: available far back in time, but treating every patent as equal, so a firm with a hundred trivial patents outscores the firm holding the one patent that defined a decade. The second generation, the workhorse of the field, counts **forward citations** — how many later patents cite this one — which genuinely tracks importance (the lineage runs through the NBER Patent Data Project of Hall, Jaffe & Trajtenberg). But citations are *slow*, accruing over a decade so a recent patent cannot be valued in real time, and *truncated*, since recent cohorts mechanically show fewer citations simply because less future has elapsed — making cross-cohort comparison the classic apples-to-oranges error.

KPSS's move, which this paper imitates, is to let a third party that has already done the valuation work — the stock market — supply the number. When the U.S. Patent and Trademark Office grants a patent to a publicly traded firm, the grant is *news*: it resolves uncertainty about whether and on what claims the patent would issue. If the patent is valuable, the firm's stock should jump when the grant becomes public; if it is worthless, the stock should not move. KPSS therefore read the abnormal return in a short window around the grant date as the market's dollar-denominated estimate of the patent's private value, and released the resulting patent-level estimates publicly, which is why the measure has become standard infrastructure across finance, economics, and management. This is the same event-study logic Week 4 applied to corporate events; here the "event" is a patent grant, and the abnormal return *is* the valuation. My contribution relative to KPSS is not a new measure but a *transparent reconstruction*: by building it on data with a known answer, I show exactly how much signal the method recovers and how much it loses — which the real measure can never reveal, because the truth is unobserved.

## 3. Data

The analysis runs on a **seeded synthetic panel** generated in `nb6.1` (random generator `np.random.default_rng(42)`), designed to mirror the schema of the real pull it stands in for. I simulate 60 firms over four calendar years, 2018–2021, at a daily frequency of roughly 252 trading days per year. Each firm has a market capitalization drawn on a log scale (median near \$2,000m, heavily right-skewed), a market beta near one, and an idiosyncratic daily volatility between 1.0% and 2.0%. Daily returns follow a market model, $R_{it} = a_i + b_i R_{mt} + \varepsilon_{it}$, the same normal-return model the event study will invert.

Onto this return panel I graft **633 patents**, roughly two to three per firm-year, each carrying a grant date and a *true* dollar value drawn from a right-skewed log-normal distribution whose scale rises with a latent firm-quality term. The resulting value distribution is wildly skewed in exactly the way real patent values are: the top 10% of patents hold **59%** of the total planted value — the blockbuster phenomenon that is the entire motivation for not counting patents equally. Each patent's true value is then injected into the data as a small abnormal return spread over its three-day grant window, scaled so that a fixed dollar value is a large percentage move for a small firm and a tiny one for a giant (a \$50m patent moves a \$200m firm by 25% but a \$20,000m firm by 0.25%). This is the mechanism the event study must run in reverse.

The synthetic panel deliberately reproduces the **selection** structure of the real data. Only patents whose firm has enough pre-grant return history to estimate a market model receive a value; the rest drop out, exactly as private firms, foreign assignees, and individual inventors drop out of the real measure for lack of a CRSP listing. After this screen, the patent-level estimates aggregate to a **217-row firm-year innovation-value panel**, the central data object.

In a real build, the data would come from three stitched-together sources, documented in the data cards and in a non-executed (`RUN_REAL=False`) cell of `nb6.1`. Patents, grant dates, and citations would come from **USPTO PatentsView** via the **USPTO Open Data Portal** (`api.uspto.gov`, `X-API-KEY` header; PatentsView migrated to ODP on March 20, 2026), or the bulk PatentsView tables on ODP for panel-scale work. The patent-to-firm link and the published per-patent values would come from the **NBER-lineage crosswalk and the public KPSS patent-value file** (bulk downloads, no key). Daily firm returns would come from **CRSP**, which is licensed and must stay read-only on GMU infrastructure with a pinned snapshot date (CONVENTIONS §5). The join chain is PatentsView patent → crosswalk → `permno`/`gvkey` → CRSP/Compustat → firm outcomes. I name this pipeline so the template is reproducible, but I do not execute it, and no real number appears anywhere in this paper.

## 4. Empirical design

### 4.1 Constructing the measure

The measure is built one patent at a time with a market-model event study, the Week-4 machine pointed at a patent grant. For patent $j$ owned by firm $i$ and granted on trading day $t_0$:

1. **Estimate the normal-return model.** On a pre-event estimation window of 120 trading days, ending 10 trading days before the grant (the gap prevents the event from leaking into the parameters), fit $R_{it} = \hat a_i + \hat b_i R_{mt}$ by least squares.
2. **Compute the abnormal return.** On each day $t$ of the three-day grant window (days $t_0$ through $t_0+2$), the abnormal return is $AR_{it} = R_{it} - (\hat a_i + \hat b_i R_{mt})$, the part of the return the market model did not predict.
3. **Cumulate and convert to dollars.** Sum the window abnormal returns into a cumulative abnormal return, $\mathrm{CAR}_{ij} = \sum_{t=t_0}^{t_0+2} AR_{it}$, and multiply by the firm's market capitalization to turn a percentage into a dollar value: $\widehat{V}_{ij} = \mathrm{CAR}_{ij}\times \text{MktCap}_i$.
4. **Aggregate.** Sum the (positive) per-patent dollar values within each firm and year to form the firm-year innovation-value panel.

The one-line specification, in the camp's discipline (CONVENTIONS §4): **outcome** (constructed) = dollar value per patent; **key input** = idiosyncratic stock return in the three-day grant window; **adjustments** = market-model-filtered, scaled by market cap; **sample** = patents granted to firms with usable pre-grant return history; **the identifying assumption, in one sentence** = *the abnormal stock return in the three-day grant window equals the market's unbiased estimate of the patent's economic value, because markets impound the patent's value at grant and no other value-relevant news contaminates the window.*

For the **predictive** validation, the specification is a firm-year regression: **outcome** = next-year firm profitability; **key regressor** = log of this year's innovation value; **controls** = log market capitalization; **fixed effects** = year; **standard errors** = classical (the synthetic panel is independent across firms by construction, so there is no within-firm clustering to correct; on real data this regression would cluster by firm, per CONVENTIONS §3); **sample** = the 217-row firm-year panel. This relationship is **predictive, not causal** — I do not claim innovation *causes* profits, only that the measure forecasts them the way a valid value measure should.

### 4.2 Threats to identification

The identifying assumption has three soft joints, each inherited directly from KPSS's "what's vulnerable." Table 1 names each threat, its direction, and the design response.

**Table 1 — Threats to the grant-window value measure, and responses**

| Threat | Why it biases the measure | Direction | Design response |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Confounding window news** (earnings, deals, analyst revisions landing in the 3-day window) | The CAR mixes the patent's value with unrelated news, so $\widehat{V}$ no longer isolates the patent | Either sign per patent; attenuates the measure on average if idiosyncratic | Short (3-day) window limits exposure; firm-year aggregation averages out *idiosyncratic* confounds; quantified in §6.3 |
| **Selection to granted patents of public firms** | Only granted patents of firms with return history get a value; rejected/abandoned patents and private, foreign, university assignees are invisible | Bounds the population, not the within-sample estimate | Disclosed, not corrected; restricts external validity (stated in §7) |
| **Expectations, not realized value** | The window return is the market's *forecast* at grant, not what the patent ultimately earned; bias if the market is systematically optimistic about flashy patents | Inflates value of patents the market over-rates | None available within the design; the measure is "market's expected value at grant," and prose says so |
| **Efficient-markets assumption baked in** | The measure assumes prices impound value promptly; if markets are slow the window misses leaked value | Attenuates | Short window plus sensitivity to window length (§6.1); cannot test efficiency with a measure that assumes it |

The first threat is the one I can manipulate in a synthetic world, so it is where §6.3 aims its experiment. The remaining three I disclose and bound rather than solve, which is the honest posture for a measurement design.

## 5. Results

The headline result is the validation table: does the measure, reconstructed from three-day stock blips, both recover the planted truth and predict future outcomes? Table 2 reports both checks, with the true-value regression as a benchmark ceiling.

**Table 2 — Recovery of the planted value and prediction of next-year profitability**

| | Recovery: corr(true, estimated) | | Prediction: next-year profitability on log innovation value | | |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| | Level | Log | $\hat\beta$ | SE | $t$ |
| **Estimated measure** (event-study CAR × mktcap) | 0.565 | 0.382 | 0.0054 | 0.0015 | 3.61 |
| Firm-year aggregate (estimated) | 0.722 | — | | | |
| *Benchmark: true planted value* | *1.000* | *1.000* | *0.0152* | *0.0017* | *9.20* |

*Notes.* Synthetic data from `nb6.1`; **illustrative, not empirical**. 633 patents and a 217-row firm-year panel, 60 firms × 2018–2021. "Recovery" columns report the Pearson correlation between the event-study-estimated patent value and the known planted true value, in levels and in logs (logs computed only where the estimated value is positive, since a CAR can come out negative). The firm-year row aggregates patent values within firm-year before correlating. The prediction columns report a firm-year OLS of next-year profitability on log innovation value, controlling for log market cap and year fixed effects; the coefficient on log innovation value is shown. Standard errors are classical (firms are independent by construction in the synthetic panel; a real-data version would cluster by firm). The benchmark row substitutes the *true* planted value for the estimated one, giving the ceiling the estimate would hit with zero measurement error. $^{\*\*\*}\,p<0.01$. Coefficient $0.0054$ has $t=3.61$, $p=0.0004$.

Three readings. First, **the event study recovers the truth, imperfectly but unmistakably.** The patent-level correlation of 0.565 in levels (0.382 in logs) is far from perfect, and it *should* be: the median patent's true value is a window blip of well under 1%, while a firm's three-day idiosyncratic noise runs several percent, so any single window is a noisy read. This is not a flaw to apologize for; it is the realistic signal-to-noise ratio that *motivates* the next number.

Second, **aggregation is the cure for window noise.** Summing patents to the firm-year lifts the recovery correlation to 0.722, because the idiosyncratic noise in individual windows is mean-zero and cancels when many patents are pooled, while the signal accumulates. This is the mechanical reason KPSS-style measures are most trustworthy at the firm-year level — the level at which the economics is actually done — and least trustworthy as a single patent's headline dollar figure.

Third, **the measure predicts the future the way a valid value measure should.** The estimated log innovation value carries a positive, significant coefficient on next-year profitability ($\hat\beta = 0.0054$, $t = 3.61$). The benchmark row shows the ceiling: with the *true* value substituted in, the coefficient is larger ($0.0152$) and the $t$-statistic far higher ($9.20$). The gap between the two rows is precisely the cost of measurement error — the estimated measure attenuates the relationship toward zero, exactly as classical measurement error in a regressor predicts, but does not destroy it. A three-day stock reaction, filtered and scaled, forecasts a real outcome a year out.

## 6. Robustness

A measurement design earns trust by surviving perturbations of the choices that built it. Three checks: the window length, a placebo window, and the confounding-news experiment that is the point of working in a sandbox.

### 6.1 Window length

The three-day window is a choice, and the right way to disclose a choice is to vary it. Shortening to a one-day window $(0,0)$ throws away abnormal return that lands on days $t_0+1$ and $t_0+2$, attenuating the measure; lengthening to a five-day window $(0,4)$ admits more unrelated news, raising the variance of the estimate and inviting the confounding-window threat of §6.3. The three-day window is the standard compromise — long enough to capture a reaction that does not all land on the grant date, short enough to keep the confound window narrow. The qualitative lesson, which `nb6.1`'s *Your Turn* extension makes a student reproduce, is that the recovery correlation is *not invariant* to the window; reporting the measure without disclosing the window would hide a real degree of freedom.

### 6.2 Placebo window

The cleanest falsification is a placebo: run the identical event study on a window where *no* patent value was injected — a stretch of trading days unconnected to any grant. By construction the synthetic data plant value only in true grant windows, so a placebo window should return a CAR that is mean-zero noise and an estimated value indistinguishable from zero, with no correlation to any patent's planted value. A placebo that instead produced apparent "value" would signal that the event-study machine was manufacturing signal out of ordinary volatility — the single most important thing to rule out before trusting the real-window estimates. The design passes this check by construction here; on real data it is the test that separates a genuine grant-window reaction from a mechanical artifact, and it is the one I would run first.

### 6.3 Contaminating the grant windows

The headline robustness exercise attacks the design at its weakest joint (Table 1, row 1): confounding news in the window. I inject a simulated one-day earnings surprise — a normal shock with a 6% standard deviation, large relative to the patent's own window signal — into a rising fraction of grant windows, rebuild the measure on the contaminated data, and watch both the recovery correlation and the predictive $t$-statistic degrade. Results are in Table 3, averaged over five contamination seeds for smoothness.

**Table 3 — Confounding window news degrades both recovery and prediction**

| Share of windows contaminated | 0% | 15% | 30% | 50% | 75% |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Recovery correlation (log) | 0.382 | 0.353 | 0.330 | 0.291 | 0.253 |
| Validation $t$ (next-year profit) | 3.61 | 3.40 | 3.13 | 2.81 | 2.45 |

*Notes.* Synthetic data from `nb6.1`; **illustrative, not empirical**. A one-day earnings surprise (Gaussian, SD = 6%) is added to the first day of a random fraction of grant windows; the value measure is then rebuilt from the contaminated returns. "Recovery correlation (log)" is corr(true, estimated) at the patent level; "Validation $t$" is the $t$-statistic on log innovation value in the next-year-profitability regression of Table 2. Each column averages five contamination seeds. The 0% column reproduces the clean estimates of Table 2.

The pattern is monotone and interpretable. As contamination rises from 0% to 75%, the recovery correlation falls from 0.382 to 0.253 and the predictive $t$-statistic falls from 3.61 to 2.45 — the measure is being eaten by noise the event study cannot distinguish from the patent's own signal. Two lessons follow. First, this is *why* the short window and firm-year aggregation are the defenses: a narrow window gives confounds less room to land, and aggregation averages out *idiosyncratic* confounds like a one-off earnings surprise. Second, and this is the subtler point KPSS's "what's vulnerable" insists on, aggregation only saves the measure when the confound is idiosyncratic. The dangerous case — flagged in `nb6.1`'s extension and not run here — is *systematic* contamination, where good-news days correlate with grant dates; that bias does not wash out under aggregation and would survive into the firm-year panel.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper reconstructs a KPSS-style, dollar-denominated innovation measure on synthetic data with a known answer, and uses that answer to show three things the real measure can never directly show: the event study *recovers* the planted patent value (correlation 0.57 at the patent level, 0.72 once aggregated to the firm-year), the measure *predicts* next-year profitability ($t = 3.6$, against a true-value ceiling of $t = 9.2$), and confounding window news *degrades* both recovery and prediction in a monotone, quantifiable way. The exercise turns an unobservable — the economic value of an idea — into an observable, and it locates the exact seams where the construction can fail.

The limits are real and I state them plainly. **The measure is the market's *expected* value at grant, not realized value;** a patent the market loved at grant and that later flopped still scores high, and any sentence dropping the words "expected" and "the market's" overclaims. **The measure assumes efficient markets,** so it cannot be used to *test* efficiency without circularity, and it is sensitive to the choice of normal-return model and window length (§6.1). **The sample is selected** to granted patents of firms with usable return history; the real-data analogue excludes private firms, foreign assignees, universities, and de-listed firms, so the firm-year panel describes the innovation of large, listed, surviving firms and nothing I show here generalizes to the whole economy. And the **predictive** validation is an association, not a causal effect — innovating firms grow and the measure forecasts it, but I have no random assignment of patents, no instrument, no discontinuity, and reverse causality (growing firms patent more) and common drivers (a demand shock lifting both) remain live alternatives. Above all, **the data are synthetic.** The numbers demonstrate that the *method* recovers a known truth and breaks under a known confound; they say nothing about real patents, real firms, or real returns. That separation — between validating a method and discovering a fact — is the discipline the whole exercise is meant to teach.

## How this paper was built — annotated margin commentary

*This section is not part of the paper. It is a build log for the next student, written to the camp's reflective standard, explaining the choices behind the paper above so the template can be reused rather than copied.*

**Why this design, and where it sits in the course.** The whole paper is the Week-4 event study (Chapter 4) pointed at a new kind of event — a patent grant instead of an earnings announcement — to build the Week-1 thing the field had despaired of: a *measure* of an unobservable (Chapter 6.1's "measuring an idea"). I chose the KPSS construction because it is the clearest case in modern finance of converting a construct (the economic value of an invention) into a measure (a three-day price reaction), and because the Week-6 Reader's Guide had already dissected its strengths and vulnerabilities, giving me a ready-made threats table to populate (Table 1). The paper structure follows Week-8 Chapter 8.3 exactly: title and abstract, an introduction that runs hook → one-sentence contribution → "I find" → roadmap, a positioning section, data, design with the identifying assumption stated in one sentence, results led by a stand-alone table, robustness, and a conclusion that ends on honest limits.

**Why synthetic data, and why label it five times.** Real CRSP returns are licensed and must stay on GMU infrastructure (CONVENTIONS §5), so a laptop-reproducible exemplar has to run on a synthetic stand-in. But the deeper reason is pedagogical: simulating the data lets me *plant a known true patent value* and then check whether the event study recovers it — something no empirical paper can do, because the truth is unobserved. That is the single most valuable thing this exercise teaches, so the synthetic nature is flagged in a banner at the top, in the abstract, in every table note, and twice in the conclusion. Over-labeling is deliberate: the failure mode I most wanted to prevent is a future reader lifting "$t = 3.6$" as if it described real firms. The numbers are all traceable to `nb6.1` (60 firms, 633 patents, 217 firm-years, recovery 0.565/0.382/0.722, validation $\hat\beta=0.0054$/$t=3.61$, degradation 0.382→0.253 and $t$ 3.61→2.45 across 0–75% contamination, top 10% holding 59% of value); I did not invent a single figure.

**How the tables follow Appendix D.** Table 2 is the headline, and it is built to stand alone (D.1 §6, D.2 §5): a reader covering the prose can still read off the dependent variable, the sample and its size, the estimator, the standard-error flavor, and what the $t$-column means, all from the note. I report the *key coefficient* (log innovation value) and one interpretable control regime, not a kitchen sink (D.2 §1). Crucially, I included the **true-value benchmark row** so the reader sees the ceiling and reads the estimated coefficient as *attenuated by measurement error* rather than as a clean effect — that contrast is the honest way to report a constructed measure. Standard errors are classical here and I say *why* in the note (firms are independent by construction in the synthetic panel), while flagging that real data would cluster by firm (CONVENTIONS §3); pretending to cluster on independent simulated data would be a false signal of rigor. I also kept significant digits matched and honest (D.1 §4): three decimals on the correlations, four on the small profitability coefficient because its standard error is uncertain in the fourth place, and no spurious precision dressed up to look authoritative.

**Why the recovery correlation is deliberately imperfect.** A student's first instinct is to read 0.565 as a disappointing number and try to "fix" it. That instinct is exactly backwards, and the paper is structured to correct it. The median patent's true value is a window blip well under 1%, while a firm's three-day idiosyncratic return noise runs several percent — so a single window is, honestly, a noisy read, and a recovery correlation near 0.4–0.57 is the *realistic* signal-to-noise ratio, not a bug to engineer away. The pedagogical payoff is the jump to 0.722 once patents are aggregated to the firm-year: that is the whole reason KPSS-style measures are trusted at the firm-year level and distrusted as a single patent's headline dollar figure, and showing both numbers side by side makes the lesson land harder than any caveat sentence could. I resisted the temptation to tune the noise floor down to produce a flattering correlation; the imperfection *is* the content.

**Calibrating the verbs (D.5).** The hardest discipline was language. The recovery and prediction results are *predictive*, not causal, so the verbs are "recovers," "predicts," "forecasts," "is associated with" — never "causes" or "leads to." The contribution sentence in §1 says what is now *shown* (that the method recovers a known truth and breaks under a known confound), not merely what I *did*, and its verb is capped by the weakest assumption. I ran the verb-audit D.5 prescribes — reading only the verbs attached to the headline result across all sections — to catch drift between the abstract and the conclusion, the place calibration usually fails. The conclusion's honest-limits paragraph is the residual-concern column of Table 1 turned into prose: expectations-not-realized-value, efficient-markets circularity, selection, and the predictive-not-causal caveat each get one plain sentence.

**What I would do with real data, and what I deliberately left out.** The real pull is documented but not run: PatentsView (now served from the USPTO Open Data Portal, `api.uspto.gov`, post the March 20, 2026 cutover) for patents and grant dates, the NBER-lineage crosswalk and public KPSS file for the firm link and published values, CRSP for daily returns on GMU infrastructure with a pinned snapshot. On real data the first thing I would add is the **placebo window** as a live test rather than a by-construction pass (§6.2), and the most important extension is the **systematic-confound** case — contamination correlated with grant dates — which does *not* wash out under aggregation and is the genuine danger the random-contamination sweep cannot show. I left those as stated extensions rather than faking them, because the line between a result I produced and one I only described is exactly the line this paper is trying to teach a student to respect. The Hall–Jaffe–Trajtenberg working paper is cited at its canonical NBER WP id (no journal volume/issue exists for a WP); where any other citation could not be verified it is tagged `[CHECK]` per CONVENTIONS §6 — a visible flag of honesty beats a confident invention every time.
